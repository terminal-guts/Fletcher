# AEC Subsector Analysis: Where Great & Underappreciated Converge

Map of which architecture, engineering, and construction subsectors are most likely to harbor genuinely great but underappreciated firms. Based on: moat strength, barriers to entry, geographic fragmentation, media/award visibility, and recent market transitions.

## Scoring Framework

Rate each subsector on five dimensions (1-5 scale):

1. **Moat Strength** (1=commoditized, 5=deep craft moat)
2. **Geographic Fragmentation** (1=national consolidation, 5=deeply local)
3. **Award & Media Visibility** (1=saturated, 5=overlooked)
4. **Recent Market Transition** (1=stable, 5=rapidly changing)
5. **Capacity for Growth** (1=saturated, 5=room to grow)

**High signal zones:** Subsectors scoring 18+ across the five dimensions.

---

## High-Signal Subsectors (18+)

### Institutional Architecture (Universities, K-12, Healthcare)
- **Moat Strength:** 4 (deep understanding of program, compliance, learning science)
- **Geographic Fragmentation:** 4 (regional expertise matters; state-level education variations)
- **Award & Media Visibility:** 3 (some AIA awards; less glamorous than residential/commercial)
- **Recent Market Transition:** 4 (K-12 needs modernization; universities rapidly changing campus needs post-COVID; healthcare shifting to ambulatory)
- **Capacity for Growth:** 5 (institutions have money, long project timelines, long-term relationships; firms often specialized and not aggressively marketed)
- **TOTAL: 20**

**Why:** Institutional clients have real money, long-term planning, and are usually loyal to one firm. Great firms in this space are often known to peer institutions but invisible nationally. Recent transitions (post-COVID campus rethinks, K-12 modernization mandates) create visibility gaps. Few firms specialize; most are generalists trying to capture it.

**Geography:** Texas has strong university/K-12 sector; Houston, Austin, Dallas all have growing populations = growing institutional need.

---

### Commercial / Mixed-Use Development (Specialty)
- **Moat Strength:** 4 (developer relationships, entitlement expertise, complex program management)
- **Geographic Fragmentation:** 4 (local regulatory and political context is everything)
- **Award & Media Visibility:** 3 (glossy renderings but little depth; award circuit favors residential)
- **Recent Market Transition:** 5 (post-COVID remote work shifting office needs; retail dying; mixed-use rising as a bet)
- **Capacity for Growth:** 4 (developers are hungry for partners; many firms are relationship-dependent and underselling)
- **TOTAL: 20**

**Why:** Developers often work with the same 3-4 architects on repeat (high retention). Great firms in this space command deep respect from developers but are invisible to the public and to other sectors. Recent transition (office → mixed-use → adaptability) has created a window where some firms are ahead of the curve but underpositioning it.

**Geography:** Major developers (real estate players) cluster in major metros. Texas has significant commercial development activity; Austin/Dallas/Houston are growth markets.

---

### Engineering (Specialized: MEP, Structural, Civil)
- **Moat Strength:** 5 (deep technical expertise, code knowledge, relationships with equipment manufacturers)
- **Geographic Fragmentation:** 4 (local regulatory variations; relationships matter)
- **Award & Media Visibility:** 5 (structural engineering gets almost zero media; MEP is invisible to public)
- **Recent Market Transition:** 4 (climate/resilience retrofit demand rising; building science becoming critical)
- **Capacity for Growth:** 4 (most firms are capacity-constrained but known only to architects/developers, not end-clients)
- **TOTAL: 22**

**Why:** Engineering firms have the deepest moats (you can't learn what a 30-year structural engineer knows in 3 years). Award circuits favor architects, not engineers. Firms doing innovative structural solutions or climate resilience work are respected by peer architects but unknown to building owners. Recent shift to resilience/net-zero creates window where some firms are ahead but underpositioning.

**Subsets:** 
- Structural: Deep moat, high technical bar, small market, relationship-driven
- MEP: Increasingly complex (building automation, renewable integration), high moat, invisible to public
- Civil: Infrastructure booming (federal funding), quality firms undersold

**Geography:** Large metros with complex development + resilience requirements (Houston for flood resilience, Austin for sustainability, Dallas for rapid growth).

---

### Design Build / Build-to-Suit Specialist Firms
- **Moat Strength:** 4 (speed, process integration, client relationships, repeat execution)
- **Geographic Fragmentation:** 4 (local market knowledge critical)
- **Award & Media Visibility:** 4 (design-build doesn't win design awards; execution excellence is invisible)
- **Recent Market Transition:** 4 (integrated delivery models growing; clients want faster, more predictable projects)
- **Capacity for Growth:** 4 (often fully booked; visible to clients in their sector, invisible outside)
- **TOTAL: 20**

**Why:** Firms that execute design-build projects reliably get repeat work but don't enter award circuits. They're valued by industrial/corporate clients but unknown to architects or the design press. Growth comes from expanding into new sectors or geographies, which is hard when visibility is limited to past client networks.

**Geography:** Industrial hubs (Houston has petrochemical, manufacturing base); growing tech facilities (Austin, Dallas).

---

### Adaptive Reuse / Historic Preservation
- **Moat Strength:** 5 (specialized knowledge, code exceptions, material sourcing, regulatory navigation)
- **Geographic Fragmentation:** 4 (historic preservation rules vary by jurisdiction)
- **Award & Media Visibility:** 3 (preservation work gets awards; reuse is trendy; but visibility is still niche)
- **Recent Market Transition:** 5 (infill/adaptive reuse exploding as development strategy; suburban retail dying)
- **Capacity for Growth:** 5 (market is undersupplied; many firms lack the expertise; growth potential huge)
- **TOTAL: 22**

**Why:** Adaptive reuse is becoming the dominant infill strategy in mature metros. Firms with deep preservation and reuse expertise have a moat (complexity of code exceptions, material science, phasing). Award circuit does recognize this work, but market demand is outrunning supply. Great firms in this space are fully booked but not aggressively seeking growth or expanding into new geographies.

**Geography:** Cities with aging infrastructure and historic cores (Houston, Austin, Dallas all have adaptive reuse momentum). Texas also has strong real estate development activity pushing infill strategies.

## Initial Research Priorities

Based on AIA conference fresh relationships and subsector analysis, prioritize:

1. **Institutional Architecture** (universities, K-12) — deep moat, regional focus, recent transition window, known in academic circles, invisible broadly.
2. **Engineering Specialists** (structural, MEP for resilience/net-zero) — extreme moat, invisible to public, recent transition creates positioning gap.
3. **Adaptive Reuse / Infill Specialists** — undersupplied market, deep expertise moat, growth window.
4. **Commercial / Mixed-Use with Developer Focus** — relationship-driven, undermarketed, recent transition (post-COVID space rethink).

**Skip initially:**
- High-end residential (already saturated with visibility)
- General practice firms (no defensible focus)

## Research Questions for Your Agents

For each high-signal subsector, your agents should answer:

1. **Which firms in this subsector are genuinely great?** (Use the scorecard to evaluate 3-5 candidates per subsector)
2. **Which are most underappreciated?** (Focus on the gap between quality and visibility)
3. **What's the recent transition creating a window?** (Post-COVID? New regulations? Changing client needs?)
4. **Who are the "qualified buyers" for this work?** (Which AIA contacts are developers, institutional decision-makers, or corporate facility managers in this space?)
