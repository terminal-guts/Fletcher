## The Two-Dimensional Gate

Every firm must pass BOTH:

| Great | Underappreciated |
|-------|------------------|
| Real moat (hard to replicate) | Weak public presence vs. work quality |
| Practitioners first | Known locally, invisible broadly |
| High retention | Referral-dependent, not saturated |
| Unpopular decisions | Peer recognition > market recognition |
| Consistent culture | Pricing doesn't match quality |
| They say no | Recent transition opens window |

**Pass threshold:** 10+ points on each (20+ total on 0-24 scale)

**Key signal:** Do they have decision authority, active pipeline, and openness to new relationships?

## The Buyer Checklist: Qualified vs. Not

✓ **Qualified:**
- Real decision authority (hires firms independently)
- Active projects now or in next 12 months
- Values quality over cost
- Open to new relationships
- Potential for repeat work

✗ **Not Qualified:**
- Only advisory role
- "We work with the same firms every time"
- Cost is the biggest factor
- Long RFP process
- One-time work only

## Key Principles

1. **Depth over speed.** 2.5 hours per firm. Signal > speed.
2. **Specificity over templates.** Research note should reference their actual work, not generic advice.
3. **Conviction matters.** If you don't believe it, they'll sense it.
4. **The gap is everything.** What's the one non-obvious observation that makes them a candidate?
5. **Buyer first, if possible.** Approach with proof of demand, not a pitch.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Skipping research.** "I can score them from a quick website look." → No. 2.5 hours minimum.
- **Confusing great with successful.** Successful ≠ great. A big firm might be great; a small firm might not be.
- **Missing the gap.** If you can't articulate the quality vs. recognition mismatch, keep digging.
- **Forcing a buyer match.** Wrong buyer + right firm = wasted credibility.
- **Approaching without conviction.** If the research didn't produce belief, move to next candidate.
