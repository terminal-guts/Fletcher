# Candidate Evaluation Scorecard

Operationalizes the strategy's 6 "great" + 6 "underappreciated" criteria into a quick assessment tool.

## Scoring System

Rate each criterion 0-2:
- **0:** Signal absent or disqualifies
- **1:** Weak signal; needs verification
- **2:** Strong signal; criterion met

**Pass threshold:** 10/12 minimum across both categories. A company needs strength across both dimensions—being great alone isn't enough if they're already appreciated, and underappreciated alone means they might not be worth the effort.

---

## Great (6 criteria, max 12 points)

### 1. Moat / Hard to Replicate
Does the work have a defensible edge?

- **2:** Protected by craft mastery, specialized relationships, or judgment that takes 5+ years to rebuild. Specific trade secrets or methods. "No, you can't poach talent and replicate us in three years."
- **1:** Some proprietary knowledge or client lock-in, but unclear how durable it is.
- **0:** Commoditized service; a well-funded competitor could catch up in 12-18 months.

*Look for:* Award citations of "innovative approach," client retention rates, specialized certifications or credentials, long-term client relationships, specific methodologies mentioned in interviews/writing.

---

### 2. Principals Are Practitioners First
Do the leaders do the work? Can they point to specific decisions shaped by craft judgment?

- **2:** Principals actively involved in project delivery. Specific, precise opinions about craft. Frustrated by industry shortcuts. Evidence they personally made hard calls on design/execution.
- **1:** Principals involved but somewhat removed; speak in general terms about process.
- **0:** Suit-focused; little hands-on work or craft language.

*Look for:* Project credits with principal names, award submission language, interview tone (do they talk about *how* things are made?), social media showing active involvement.

---

### 3. Retention & Referral
Do clients come back unprompted?

- **2:** Portfolio shows repeated work with same clients over 5+ years. Multiple projects. Evidence of "repeat customer" relationships.
- **1:** Some repeat clients, but also churny; mix of one-off and recurring.
- **0:** Transactional client base; portfolio is all different names.

*Look for:* Project history (same client names recurring?), case study language ("we've worked with X for 15 years"), referral-dependent positioning.

---

### 4. Right But Unpopular Decisions
Have they made a bet on X when everyone was doing Y?

- **2:** Clear pivot or conviction call against industry consensus. Award citations or interviews acknowledging the counterintuitive choice.
- **1:** Some evidence of independent thinking, but not a bold bet.
- **0:** Follows the trend; no evidence of conviction-driven decisions.

*Look for:* Award submission text (often highlights "dared to..."), interviews discussing why they chose an approach others didn't, decisions mentioned in peer reviews as "ahead of its time," regulatory or project changes they pushed for.

---

### 5. Consistent Culture Top to Bottom
Does the firm's identity remain consistent from principal to junior?

- **2:** Junior staff describe the firm similarly to principals. Consistent values in award submissions and social media. Turnover is low or by choice (retirement, moves).
- **1:** Culture seems intact but hard to verify; little public evidence.
- **0:** Inconsistent messaging; junior staff and principals describe different firms.

*Look for:* Employee tenure and Glassdoor reviews, what staff say in interviews/podcasts, social media from non-principals, how the firm describes itself across different contexts.

---

### 6. They Say No
Do they turn down clients, projects, or fee structures that imply compromise?

- **2:** Explicit examples of declined work due to values/fit. "We passed on X because it didn't align with Y." Confident pricing that excludes low-fee work.
- **1:** Some evidence they're selective, but vague; no clear examples.
- **0:** Takes most work; no evidence of saying no.

*Look for:* Project selection (gaps in the portfolio that might be refusals), pricing positioning (do they compete on cost or quality?), interviews where they discuss "clients we decided not to work with," size of team relative to inquiry volume.

---

## Underappreciated (6 criteria, max 12 points)

### 1. Weak Public Presence Relative to Work Quality
Is the firm invisible online compared to the quality of their work?

- **2:** Portfolio is excellent; web presence is outdated, understated, or sparse. No active press outreach. Minimal social media.
- **1:** Adequate but dated website; some press coverage, but gaps.
- **0:** Strong web/social presence that reflects the work quality; visible enough already.

*Look for:* Website aesthetics and update frequency, Google/media mentions, social media activity, press releases issued, award entries (or lack thereof).

---

### 2. Known Locally, Invisible Broadly
Are they the "go-to" in one region/segment but unknown elsewhere?

- **2:** Strong local reputation (Houston, Austin, etc.) or segment (residential, institutional); zero presence nationally. Search results show local mentions only.
- **1:** Known in one market; some spillover nationally.
- **0:** Already has national visibility; name carries weight across regions.

*Look for:* Where project references come from (all same state/city?), where their awards are from (local chapter awards vs. AIA national?), who they name as competitors (all local or national?), award submission geography.

---

### 3. Referral-Dependent but Not Saturated
Are they growing slowly due to visibility, not capacity?

- **2:** "We have room to take on more work; we just need the right clients." Growing slowly. Capacity >80%. Not on the radar of large corporations or institutional buyers.
- **1:** Some capacity, some growth limitations unclear.
- **0:** Fully booked or declining work volume; capacity constraint is the limiting factor.

*Look for:* Interview language about growth, pipeline discussion, how they describe client acquisition ("referral-based"), team size vs. revenue, timeline for new projects.

---

### 4. Peer Recognition Exceeds Market Recognition
Are industry insiders impressed while the broader market sleeps on them?

- **2:** Multiple AIA/chapter awards, cited by peer firms, featured in trade publications; but marketing/press is weak. Industry peers get it, corporate buyers don't.
- **1:** Some peer recognition; some public visibility.
- **0:** Equal recognition from peers and market; already well-known.

*Look for:* Award history (especially chapter/AIA vs. broad marketing), who cites them (other architects? thought leaders?), design publication features, speaking invitations, industry association roles.

---

### 5. Pricing Doesn't Match Quality
Is there a gap between work quality and fee structure?

- **2:** Doing premium work at mid-market pricing. Firm reports "we could be charging more." Undercut by larger firms despite superior quality.
- **1:** Pricing is close to market; maybe slightly undervalued.
- **0:** Pricing reflects quality; already commanding premium rates.

*Look for:* Relative pricing vs. peers, what they say about pricing strategy, client segments (if doing high-end work for mid-market prices, gap is real), project complexity vs. perceived size of firm.

---

### 6. Recent Transition Creates a Window
Has a recent change opened an opportunity?

- **2:** Partner retirement, key client relationship ended, new geography/service line launch, new principal brought in. Quality unchanged; positioning hasn't caught up.
- **1:** Minor transition; unclear impact.
- **0:** No recent changes; firm is stable.

*Look for:* Leadership changes, firm website updates, new service lines, office openings, announcements, LinkedIn changes, when they published recent work.

---

## Quick Filter (Disqualifying Factors)

Stop at 0 if any of these are true:
- Founder/principal recently left with no clear succession plan
- Heavy recent turnover of key staff
- Litigation or regulatory issues
- Financial distress signals
- Poor Glassdoor/Indeed reviews from staff (especially if multiple mention culture or pay)
- Pricing strategy is to compete on cost, not quality
- Portfolio shows rapid shift in project type/quality (suggests churn or lost convictions)
