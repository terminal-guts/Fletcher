# First Approach Template

When you're ready to contact a firm, this is the structure. The goal: lead with what you noticed, connect it to their gap, make a small ask (read a document we prepared, not a meeting yet).

---

## Email Structure

**Subject:** [Observation] + [Connection]

Example: "The recognition gap at [Firm Name]" or "[Principal Name], your work in [area] and a research we're doing"

---

**Opening:** Lead with what you noticed, not a pitch about you

❌ Don't start with: "We're Fletcher, a company that helps great firms..."
✓ Do start with: "When we were at the AIA conference, we kept hearing about your work in [area]..."

Or: "We've been researching [sector], and your work on [specific project] stood out because [specific observation]."

The observation should be **non-obvious** — something you found that most people wouldn't dig for. Not: "You do good work." But: something you noticed in their portfolio, award history, or market position that signals a gap.

---

**Connect it to their gap:** Show you understand the delta

"It seems like the recognition hasn't caught up with the [quality / innovation / market position] yet."

Or: "You're known for [X] in [geography], but the broader market isn't seeing [Y], which seems like a real opportunity."

Point out the gap, not as criticism, but as opportunity.

---

**Make the first ask small:** Not a meeting. A document.

"We put together a brief on how we think about your opportunity and what we'd do if we were you. Would you be open to reading it and telling us if we've understood the situation correctly?"

Or: "We did some research on [your sector/firm] and have a perspective on your growth opportunity. Would it be worth a quick read?"

---

## The Document They'll Read

This is the accompanying "brief" you mention in the email. It's 2-4 pages. Structure:

### Page 1: The Observation
**Headline:** [Non-obvious observation about their gap]

**Why we noticed:** How you found this (conference, research, portfolio analysis, market observation)

**The gap:** Quality vs. recognition / local vs. national / capacity vs. positioning / potential vs. trajectory

### Page 2: The Opportunity
**What could change:** If they repositioned, expanded, doubled down on their strength, or solved the visibility/capacity/positioning problem, what would shift?

**Market signals:** Evidence that this opportunity is real (growing demand, sector change, pipeline expansion)

**Why now:** What's changed that opens this window? (Recent transition, market shift, capability maturation)

### Page 3: What We Think They Should Do
**If we were you, we'd:**
- [Specific action 1] — because [reason]
- [Specific action 2] — because [reason]
- [Specific action 3] — because [reason]

Don't be timid. Make a call. Show conviction.

### Page 4 (Optional): How We Can Help
**We can help by:**
- [Specific capability: research, strategy, execution, network, etc.]
- [Specific capability]

Keep it simple. Don't list a menu. Name what we'd actually do for them.

**The ask:** "We'd like to explore this together. Would you be open to a conversation?"

---

## Writing the Document: Tone & Voice

- **Specific, not generic.** Reference their actual work, not generic advice.
- **Conviction, not flattery.** "You should do X" not "You might consider maybe thinking about X."
- **Problem-focused.** Lead with the gap, not our solution. The problem is what matters.
- **Respect for craft.** Show you understand what they do and why it's hard. You're not here to tell them how to do their work.
- **Realistic about effort.** If the opportunity requires real work, say so.

---

## Pre-Approach Checklist

Before sending the email, confirm:

- [ ] **Research is deep.** 2.5+ hours on this firm. You know their portfolio, competitive set, market position.
- [ ] **Observation is non-obvious.** Would most people notice this gap? If not, you're good. If it's surface-level, dig deeper.
- [ ] **Gap is real.** Quality > recognition? Capacity > pipeline? Local > national? You can point to evidence.
- [ ] **Document is specific to them.** Not a template with their name swapped. Actual insights about their work and opportunity.
- [ ] **You have conviction.** The research produced belief, not just curiosity. Do you actually think this is a great firm and a real opportunity?
- [ ] **You have a buyer (optional but ideal).** If you can mention, "We connected you with [Buyer] who's interested in [your work]," even better.

---

## Follow-up Sequence

**If no response after 5 days:**
Send a single follow-up. Brief. "Just wanted to make sure the brief landed. Happy to discuss whenever works."

**If they respond with interest:**
Schedule a conversation. Lead with understanding, not pitching.

**If they decline or say they're not interested:**
Respect it. Note it. Move to the next candidate.

## Examples

### Example 1: Institutional Architecture Firm

**Subject:** The institution-building opportunity at [Firm Name]

> Hi [Principal Name],
>
> I was at the AIA conference in San Diego and kept hearing your name in conversations about university and K-12 master planning. When I looked at your portfolio, I saw fifteen years of work at [Client A], [Client B], [Client C]—all repeat relationships, all complex institutional programs.
>
> What struck me: you're known for this work inside the institutional world, but if you search for "[Firm Name]" most people outside that ecosystem wouldn't know what you do. The award history is there, the work is exceptional, but the visibility hasn't caught up.
>
> We did some research on where institutional architecture is heading post-COVID, and I think there's a real opportunity here. We put together a brief on what we see and what we think you should be doing differently. Would you be open to reading it and telling us if we've understood the situation?
>
> Thanks,
> Saul

**Document headline:** "Why [Firm Name] should own the institutional architecture space nationally—not just regionally"

---

### Example 2: Structural Engineering Firm

**Subject:** The resilience window at [Firm Name]

> Hi [Principal Name],
>
> Your structural work keeps coming up when we talk to architects about resilience and climate adaptation. We looked at your portfolio and noticed you've done work on [specific resilience projects] over the past 3 years—sophisticated stuff on [technical detail]. But your positioning still leads with "structural engineering" and your website barely mentions the resilience angle.
>
> Meanwhile, institutional and government clients are asking for exactly this. We think you're ahead of the market but positioned behind it.
>
> We put together a perspective on the opportunity here and what repositioning could unlock. Curious to know if you see it the same way.
>
> Saul

**Document headline:** "Structural engineering's moment: why resilience is your market"

---

## Notes

- **Timing matters.** Don't approach multiple firms from the same buyer or sector simultaneously. Pursue one. If they pass, move to the next.
- **Conviction matters most.** If you don't actually believe they're great and the gap is real, they'll sense it.
- **Gift before ask.** The research IS the gift. You've put in work. You're showing thinking. That's what makes the ask credible.
- **Speed signal.** If they respond positively, you can respond fast. Don't drag out "decision-making." Saul and Adam are ready to move.
