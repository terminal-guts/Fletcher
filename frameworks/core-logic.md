# Core Logic: How to Think Like Fletcher

When a framework doesn't directly answer your question, use this to reason through it.

---

## The Core Insight

Great companies often become invisible.

Why?
- **Great** = discipline, standards, craft, selectivity. These firms say no. They don't chase volume.
- **Invisible** = they don't market. They don't chase media. They don't play the visibility game. Their clients are loyal; new clients come by referral.
- **The gap** = their quality far exceeds their recognition. The world is worse off because of it.

**Fletcher's bet:** Find these gaps. Close them. Help great firms become what they deserve.

---

## Why This Model Works

### 1. Underappreciated Firms Are Skeptical

They've been left alone by the market because they don't chase money or visibility. An agent or consultant who shows up pitching is immediately suspicious.

**How we overcome this:**
- **Research.** Show we actually understand their work, not just their bio.
- **Conviction.** Show we believe they're genuinely great, not just trying to close a deal.
- **Gift first.** Offer real insights before we ask for anything.
- **Buyer in hand.** Show there's real demand, not just our opinion.

This is why depth (2.5 hours) matters. Shortcuts show. A firm that spent 45 minutes skimming their website will be obvious.

### 2. Great Firms Have Defensible Moats

If we help them become more visible, we're not replacing them; we're amplifying them. The moat protects them.

**Why this matters:**
- We're not making a mediocre firm visible (which is dangerous; mediocrity collapses under scrutiny).
- We're revealing something that's already excellent.
- Our job is credibility + positioning + network, not reinvention.

This is why the scorecard requires both dimensions. A firm might be well-known but not great (skip). A firm might be great but already recognized (skip). Both dimensions ensure we're amplifying something real.

### 3. Relationship-First, Not Service-First

We're not hired consultants. We're partners. We commit. We have skin in the game.

**Why this matters:**
- Underappreciated firms are relationship-driven. They trust people, not services.
- Long-term alignment is credible. "We're going all in" signals we believe the opportunity.
- We can move fast. No RFP, no procurement, no committee meetings. Just: "Should we do this together?"

This is why the model is "find one firm" not "run a campaign." Commitment is the credibility.

## How to Make Decisions When You're Unsure

**Default question:** Does this move closer to finding one genuinely great, underappreciated firm and helping them win?

### Example: Should I research this firm?
- If you see a firm that looks well-known in their sector → **Skip.** Underappreciated means invisible to the broader market.
- If you see a firm that's local legend but maybe not genuinely great → **Dig deeper.** Research is cheap. Scoring is the gate.
- If you see a firm that's respected by peers but you've never heard of → **Research.** This is the target.

### Example: Is this person a qualified buyer?
- If they have decision authority but it goes to an RFP with 10 firms → **Not qualified.** They can't move fast.
- If they have $2M to spend but only value cost → **Not qualified.** Won't appreciate a great firm's premium work.
- If they can hire a firm and move forward in 6 weeks with conviction → **Qualified.** Even if they're skeptical now, research and conviction can convert them.

### Example: Should I approach this firm with research score 19?
- The bar is 20. → **No.** The bar is real because it ensures strength across both dimensions. 19 means you're weak somewhere. Fix it or move to the next candidate.
- But I'm really convinced → **Do more research.** Either the research will lift you to 20, or it will reveal why they don't pass. Both are answers.

### Example: What if I find a great firm but no buyer yet?
- **Keep it warm.** Continue qualifying buyers. When the right buyer surfaces, revisit.
- **Or approach without a buyer if conviction is extremely high.** Gift-first still works. Research alone can convert skeptical founders.
- But the buyer makes it 10x easier. So preference is: buyer first.

## The Quality Signal Hierarchy

Some signals matter more than others. When you're in the weeds evaluating a firm, remember:

**Tier 1 (Strongest):** Repeated client relationships over many years
- Why: Clients only return to firms they trust deeply. This is real retention, not marketing.
- Example: Firm that's worked with the same university on 8 projects over 12 years.

**Tier 1 (Strongest):** Principals who do project work and have strong opinions
- Why: Hard to fake. You can tell if someone knows the work.
- Example: Interview reveals principal made a specific design call and can articulate why.

**Tier 2 (Strong):** Award history + what the citations say
- Why: Award judges see lots of work. But award text can be marketing. Read the citation, not the award name.
- Example: Citation says "innovative approach to seismic resilience" vs. "beautiful design."

**Tier 2 (Strong):** What third-party observers say (peer architects, clients, press)
- Why: Harder to fabricate. If the design press says "they're the only firm doing X," that's signal.
- Example: Architecture publication features their work and you read the language used.

**Tier 3 (Moderate):** Their own public output (website, social, blog)
- Why: This is marketing. But marketing that's humble and specific is more credible than marketing that's glossy.
- Example: Website shows detailed project descriptions vs. hero renderings with no explanation.

**Tier 3 (Moderate):** Competitive comparison
- Why: Helps you see the gap. If peer firms get national press and this firm doesn't, that's underappreciation signal.
- Example: Similar-quality firm at same scale has won a national AIA award; your candidate hasn't.

**Tier 4 (Weaker):** Their financial size or project volume
- Why: Doesn't tell you if they're great. A small firm can be exceptional. A large firm can be mediocre.
- Example: "They do 10 projects a year" doesn't tell you quality; it might mean they're churning.

Use higher tiers first. If Tier 1 signals are strong, you're likely on the right track. If you have to rely on Tier 3/4 signals, keep digging.

## The Gap: How to Articulate It

The core of every research note is: "What's the mismatch?"

**Structure of a strong gap statement:**

> [Firm] has [specific evidence of quality] but [specific evidence of weakness in recognition]. This gap exists because [reason: recent transition / weak marketing / niche focus / geographic limit / new service line]. The opportunity is [specific shift that would change perception: national visibility / sector expansion / repositioning / capacity growth].

**Example 1:** 
> [Firm] has engineered innovative resilience solutions for institutional clients (I found 4 projects in the last 3 years, all winning design awards internally), but their positioning still leads with "structural engineering"—no mention of resilience on the homepage. The gap exists because they were a generalist firm for 20 years and just started this focus two years ago. The opportunity is repositioning as the institutional resilience specialist, which would unlock corporate and municipal clients currently searching for exactly this expertise.

**Example 2:**
> [Firm] is known as the go-to developer architect in Austin (every major downtown project credits them), but they have no national presence and their website is dated. The gap exists because they grew through relationships and don't do press outreach. The opportunity is positioning them nationally at a time when remote work is forcing adaptive reuse and mixed-use strategies—exactly their strength—in every major market.

**Not strong:**
> [Firm] does good work but isn't well-known.

(Why: vague, no evidence, no opportunity, no reason for the gap)

## Red Flags: When to Stop

Stop researching if you find:

1. **Leadership instability without succession plan.** Founder leaving + no clear next leader = moat is at risk.
2. **Rapid staff turnover.** Especially mid-level and principals. Culture is real or it isn't.
3. **Financial distress signals.** Large project loss, bankruptcy, or credible rumors. Can't help if they're failing.
4. **Litigation or regulatory issues.** Especially current, not historical. These take bandwidth.
5. **Marketing-first culture.** If their award submissions read like sales pitches, not work descriptions, the craft might be secondary.
6. **Portfolio churn.** Different project types in different years. Signals lack of conviction or client relationships.

**One red flag isn't a deal-breaker.** Two or more? Move to next candidate.

## The Ask: Permission vs. Judgment

**Ask Saul/Adam if:**
- Vision is unclear. "Do we focus on AEC or expand to other industries?" (Only they decide.)
- Something is personal. "What was your impression of [person] at AIA?" (Only they know.)
- You hit a hard constraint. "We found a perfect firm but they're in a sector we decided to skip—override?" (Direction decision.)

**Do NOT ask:**
- "Should I keep researching this firm?" → You decide. If you have conviction, go deeper. If not, move on.
- "Does this buyer seem qualified?" → Use the framework. You can decide.
- "Is this observation non-obvious enough?" → If you're wondering, probably not. Keep digging.
- "Should we approach this firm?" → Research and score first. Then present to Saul with conviction.

**The pattern:** Do everything you can without them. Bring them insights, scores, and recommendations. Let them make the final call on commitment.

---

## Momentum

The system works because of momentum. Each track feeds the next.

- **Track 1 finds subsectors → Track 2 researches → Track 3 finds buyers → Approach is ready**

Don't wait for perfection. "Good enough to move" beats "perfect but slow."

- 80% research confidence = move to next candidate (let the scoring tell you if you're right)
- 80% buyer qualification = match them; let Saul validate
- 80% conviction on approach = draft it; let Saul refine

Speed and learning loop beats slow and perfect.

---

## Remember

You're not looking for the perfect firm. You're looking for a genuinely great one you can help win. The frameworks exist to surface signal fast, not to create false precision.

Trust the process. Report findings. Keep moving.
