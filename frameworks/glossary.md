# Glossary: Fletcher Core Terms

Quick reference for all key concepts. Use this when you're unsure what a term means.

---

## Core Mission

**Fletcher:** Find genuinely great but underappreciated companies and help them become what they deserve.

---

## The Evaluation System

### Great (6 criteria)

**Moat / Hard to Replicate**
- Work protected by craft mastery, specialized relationships, or judgment that takes 5+ years to rebuild
- Can't catch up with $50M in 3 years
- Example: A structural engineer who understands complex seismic resilience that you can't poach

**Principals Are Practitioners First**
- Leaders actively do the work, not just manage
- Have strong opinions about craft and execution
- Will point to specific decisions shaped by judgment, not just process
- Example: Principal architect who still designs projects and gets frustrated by industry shortcuts

**Retention & Referral**
- Clients come back unprompted
- Portfolio shows repeated work with same clients over years
- Evidence of "repeat customer" relationships, not transactional one-offs
- Example: Firm that's worked with the same university for 15+ years on multiple campus projects

**Right But Unpopular Decisions**
- Firm made a bet on X when everyone was doing Y
- Evidence of conviction-driven choices against industry consensus
- Often mentioned in award submissions as "dared to..." or "took a risk on..."
- Example: Chose to specialize in resilience when market wanted speed

**Consistent Culture Top to Bottom**
- Junior staff describe the firm the same way principals do
- Values consistent across projects, social media, award submissions
- Low turnover or turnover by choice (retirements, moves), not churn
- Example: Employee testimonials match principal vision without sounding coached

**They Say No**
- Turn down clients, projects, or fee structures that imply compromise
- Confident pricing that excludes low-fee work
- No evidence of desperation or "we'll take anything"
- Example: "We passed on that developer because the margin was too tight to do the work right"

### Underappreciated (6 criteria)

**Weak Public Presence Relative to Work Quality**
- Portfolio is excellent but web presence is outdated, understated, or sparse
- No active press outreach
- Minimal social media
- Example: Award-winning firm with a website that looks 10 years old

**Known Locally, Invisible Broadly**
- "Go-to" firm in one region or segment, but unknown outside it
- Search results show only local mentions
- Awards are local/chapter level, not national
- Example: Best institutional architect in Houston; nobody in San Francisco has heard of them

**Referral-Dependent but Not Saturated**
- Growing slowly because of visibility, not capacity constraints
- "We have room to take on more work; we just need the right clients"
- Capacity available; they're not fully booked
- Example: Firm that could double revenue if they had pipeline but isn't aggressively selling

**Peer Recognition Exceeds Market Recognition**
- Industry insiders are impressed; broader market sleeps on them
- Multiple AIA/chapter awards; cited by peer firms; featured in trade publications
- But marketing/press is weak; corporate buyers don't know them
- Example: Every architect respects them; corporate real estate teams have never heard the name

**Pricing Doesn't Match Quality**
- Doing premium work at mid-market pricing
- Firm reports "we could be charging more"
- Undercut by larger firms despite superior quality
- Example: Winning institutional projects while larger generalists in same city charge 30% more

**Recent Transition Creates a Window**
- Partner retirement, key client relationship ended, new geography/service line, new principal
- Quality unchanged; positioning hasn't caught up
- Example: Founding partner retired; firm is stronger than ever but market perception hasn't updated

## The Research System

**Candidate:** A firm being evaluated for greatness + underappreciation

**Research Workflow:** 2.5-hour deep-dive process to gather signal about a candidate firm
- Portfolio analysis (45 min)
- Third-party/market signals (45 min)
- Depth signals & gap identification (45 min)
- Scoring (15 min)

**Scorecard:** 24-point evaluation tool (0-24 points)
- 12 points for "great" (6 criteria × 2 points each)
- 12 points for "underappreciated" (6 criteria × 2 points each)
- Pass threshold: 10+ on each dimension, 20+ total

**Research Note:** Markdown file documenting findings for one candidate
- Portfolio highlights, competitive position, culture signals
- The gap (quality vs. recognition) in specific terms
- Opening observation (the non-obvious thing you'd lead with)
- Stored in `candidates/[Firm-Name].md`

## The Buyer System

**Qualified Buyer:** A decision-maker with real authority, active pipeline, and openness to new relationships
- Makes hiring decisions (not just advises)
- Has projects now or in next 12 months
- Values quality over cost
- Potential for repeat work
- Accessible (warm intro or open to conversation)

**Buyer Matching:** Process of identifying which of your AIA contacts are qualified buyers and connecting them to firms

**Buyer Matching Framework:** Tool for evaluating AIA contacts and qualifying them
- Decision authority level (High / Medium / Low)
- Active pipeline (Y/N) and project type
- Timeline (3mo / 6mo / 12mo)
- Openness to new relationships

**Buyer Database:** Documented list of all AIA relationships, qualified by buyer potential
- Stored in `buyers/[Name]-[Company].md`

## The Approach System

**The Gap:** Mismatch between a firm's quality and market recognition
- Examples: Known locally but not nationally; highly skilled but low visibility; full capacity but underpositioning
- This is what you identify in research and lead with in an approach

**Opening Observation:** The one non-obvious thing you noticed about a firm that makes them a candidate
- Not: "You do good work"
- But: "I noticed you've taken on increasingly complex institutional work but your positioning still leads with residential—seems like an earned opportunity gap"

**First Approach Document:** 2-4 page brief you send with initial outreach
- Page 1: The observation and gap
- Page 2: The opportunity (why now, market signals)
- Page 3: What you'd do if you were them (specific actions)
- Page 4 (optional): How we can help

**First Approach:** Initial email to a firm
- Lead with what you noticed (non-obvious observation)
- Connect to their gap (quality vs. recognition)
- Make a small ask (read the brief, not a meeting)

## AEC Subsectors

**AEC:** Architecture, Engineering, Construction

**High-Signal Subsectors:** Areas with high density of great + underappreciated firms
- Institutional Architecture (universities, K-12, healthcare)
- Engineering Specialists (structural, MEP, civil)
- Adaptive Reuse / Historic Preservation
- Commercial / Mixed-Use Development

**Medium-Signal:** Still viable but more competition / visibility already exists
- Residential (high-end/custom)
- Interior Design / Workplace Design
- Landscape Architecture / Urban Design

**Low-Signal:** Too commoditized or saturated; skip for now
- General practice (residential + commercial mix)
- Real estate development / project management firms

## Key Contacts & Relationships

**AIA Network:** People you met at AIA conference in San Diego (2026-06-14)
- Source of candidate firm leads
- Source of qualified buyers
- Document all relationships in buyer database

**Warm Introduction:** Connection through mutual contact or event
- Stronger than cold outreach
- Example: Met at AIA conference; they mentioned competitor; that's a warm intro

**Cold Introduction:** No prior contact or mutual connection
- Use if you have strong conviction and research
- Risk: Lower response rate

---

## Core Conviction

**The Model:** Relationship-first, conviction-driven
- Find one genuinely great firm (not a list)
- Understand them deeply (2-3 hours research minimum)
- Commit fully to helping them win
- Approach with research + a buyer to prove demand

**Why This Works:**
- Most underappreciated firms are skeptical of outsiders; conviction + research builds trust
- Approach with a buyer (not a pitch) signals skin in the game
- Depth of understanding creates credibility
- Relationship-first means long-term, not transactional

## Common Questions

**Q: What's the difference between a "great" firm and a firm we'd approach?**
A: All firms we approach must be great AND underappreciated. If they're great but already well-known, they don't need us. If they're underappreciated but not actually great, it's a waste of time.

**Q: Why 2.5 hours of research minimum?**
A: Shortcuts show. Firms can tell if you actually know their work vs. skimmed their website. The research produces conviction. No conviction = no credibility.

**Q: What if a firm scores 19 instead of 20?**
A: Move to the next candidate. The bar is real. 20+ is the threshold because it signals strength across both dimensions, not just one.

**Q: Do we need a buyer before approaching a firm?**
A: Ideally yes. The buyer is the proof of demand. Without it, it's a pitch. With it, it's an introduction with credibility.

**Q: What if we find a great firm but no matching buyer in our network yet?**
A: Document it. Keep it warm. You can approach without a buyer if conviction is high enough, but a buyer makes it stronger.

**Q: Who decides if we approach a firm?**
A: Saul and Adam. Agents research and score; Saul decides if conviction is high enough to approach.

**Q: What's the difference between this and traditional consulting?**
A: We commit to one firm, not work on campaigns with many. We're not hired to advise; we're partners in their growth. This is relationship-first, not service-first.

## Decision Flow

```
Research Candidate
       ↓
Score on Scorecard (≥20?)
       ├─ NO → Move to Next Candidate
       └─ YES ↓
Find Matching Buyer from Network
       ↓
Buyer Interested?
       ├─ NO → Move to Next Buyer or Keep Candidate Warm
       └─ YES ↓
Create Approach Document
       ↓
Send First Approach Email + Brief
       ↓
Firm Responds with Interest?
       ├─ NO → Move to Next Candidate
       └─ YES ↓
Schedule Conversation
       ↓
Saul & Adam Decide: Commit or Pass?
```
