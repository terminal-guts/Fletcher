# Buyer Matching Framework

How to identify and match qualified buyers (clients) from your AIA network to great underappreciated firms. This is the link in the chain: **find firm → find buyer → introduce buyer → approach firm with proof of demand.**

---

## What Makes a "Qualified Buyer"

A buyer who can move projects fast and commit to a firm relationship:

### Essential Attributes

1. **Real Decision Authority** — they decide which firms to hire, not just advise
2. **Active Project Pipeline** — they have projects in development now or in the next 12 months
3. **Openness to New Relationships** — willing to try a firm they've never worked with
4. **Values Quality Over Cost** — won't shop on price; will pay for the right fit
5. **Repeat Work Possibility** — potential for multiple projects, not one-time work

### Secondary Attributes (Ideal but Not Required)

- Preference for local/regional firms (aligns with how underappreciated firms operate)
- Sector alignment (institutional buyer → institutional architecture firm; developer → mixed-use firm)
- Introductory relationship with you (warm, not cold)
- Public/institutional decision-maker (universities, cities, healthcare, corporations) vs. private developer

## Buyer Types by Subsector

### For Institutional Architecture Firms

**Best Buyers:**
- University/college facilities decision-makers (capital planning, campus master planning roles)
- School district superintendents or directors of facilities
- Hospital/health system real estate/facilities leadership
- Large non-profit leaders with capital plans (museums, libraries, religious institutions)

**How to Identify at AIA:**
- Title keywords: "Facilities," "Campus," "Real Estate," "Capital Planning," "Operations," "Director of Planning," "VP of Development"
- Look for: People who manage large built environments; came to AIA for industry learning, not to talk about a specific project

**The Conversation:**
- "You manage [scope of facilities]. We've been researching architectural partners who [specific strength]. Are you looking at any new projects over the next year?"
- Listen for pipeline signals: "We're actually about to start a master plan for..." or "We've been thinking about renovating..."

**Red Flags:**
- "We work with the same firms every time" (loyalty is real)
- "We have an RFP process; it goes to a bunch of firms" (commoditized selection)

---

### For Engineering Specialist Firms (Structural, MEP, Resilience)

**Best Buyers:**
- Project developers/real estate companies (they hire architects, who hire engineers)
- Architects at larger firms (who subcontract specialized engineers)
- Facilities managers at large institutions or corporations managing existing buildings

**How to Identify at AIA:**
- Developers are often at AIA to find architects
- Facilities/operations roles managing complex buildings
- "Principal" or "Partner" level at architectural firms (decision-makers on subcontractors)

**The Conversation:**
- "We've noticed you do [project type]. For your next project, have you worked with [specialist]? They do [specific innovation] really well."
- Listen for: "We've been meaning to find someone who specializes in..." or "That's a gap we've had"

---

### For Adaptive Reuse / Infill Specialists

**Best Buyers:**
- Urban infill developers
- Real estate companies focused on existing stock (not ground-up)
- Cities/municipalities doing adaptive reuse initiatives
- Non-profit housing developers (community development)

**How to Identify at AIA:**
- Look for: People talking about urban/downtown projects, historic preservation interests
- Title keywords: "Development," "Urban," "Real Estate," "Community Development," "Placemaking"

**The Conversation:**
- "I noticed you work on infill/adaptive reuse projects. We've been tracking [firm] who does this really well. Have you worked with them or firms like them?"

---

### For Commercial / Mixed-Use Developers

**Best Buyers:**
- Commercial real estate developers (office, retail, mixed-use focus)
- Corporate facility/real estate teams (deciding on tenant improvements, HQs)
- Urban planning/municipality leaders pushing mixed-use

**How to Identify at AIA:**
- Most commercial developers at AIA are looking for architects
- Listen for: People discussing office repositioning, mixed-use strategies, corporate HQ projects

## Qualifying Call: The Five Questions

Use these to quickly assess if a buyer is qualified (ask 2-3; you'll know after):

1. **"Are you actively looking at new projects over the next 12 months?"** (Pipeline signal)
2. **"When you pick an architectural or engineering firm, who makes the decision?"** (Authority signal)
3. **"Do you usually work with the same firms, or are you open to new relationships?"** (Openness signal)
4. **"What's most important to you in a firm—reputation, cost, specific expertise, or something else?"** (Quality vs. cost signal)
5. **"If you found the right firm for [specific project type], would you move forward quickly, or does it go through a long RFP process?"** (Speed & openness signal)

**Red flags:** RFP processes, "we only work with X firms," "cost is the biggest factor," "we're not looking right now."

**Green flags:** "We're actually just starting to plan...", "we've been looking for someone who specializes in...", "good fit matters more than cost," "we can move fast if it's the right firm."

## The Buyer-Firm Introduction

Once you have both:

1. **Confirm the firm can serve the buyer** (Check scorecard + research.)
2. **Check if the buyer is truly open** (Don't introduce a reluctant buyer to a firm; wastes credibility.)
3. **Make the introduction:** Brief, specific, problem-focused.

**Email structure:**

Subject: "[Buyer Name], I want to introduce you to [Firm Name]"

> Hi [Buyer Name],
>
> I connected with you at AIA about your work on [project type]. You mentioned you're looking at [specific project].
>
> I've been tracking [Firm Name] — they do [specific thing] really well, and I think they'd be a great fit for what you're working on. I'm going to introduce you both.
>
> [Firm contact], [Buyer contact], you should talk.
>
> Saul

**That's it.** Don't pitch. Let them talk.

```
[Your 1-2 sentence impression: are they a qualified buyer?]

```

Map all AIA conference relationships through this framework. Highlight the 5-10 most qualified buyers. Then match them to firms you research.

## Win Condition

You have a qualified buyer introduction to approach a firm with when:

1. ✓ You've researched a firm and scored them 20+ on the scorecard
2. ✓ You've found a qualified buyer whose pipeline aligns with that firm's work
3. ✓ You've confirmed the buyer is open to a new relationship
4. ✓ You've made the introduction
5. ✓ The buyer shows interest in meeting with the firm

Then you approach: "We just connected you with [Buyer]. They want to talk about [project]. Let's discuss how to support that win."

This is your opening move.
