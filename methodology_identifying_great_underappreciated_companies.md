# Methodology: Identifying & Engaging Great Underappreciated Companies

**Goal:** Find genuinely great companies stuck for fixable reasons—companies that deserve to be winning but aren't, due to a specific blocking factor that Saul's team can help address. Then approach and engage them with a clear value proposition.

**Scope:** Full end-to-end workflow from sourcing through engagement. This is the playbook. Update as we learn what works.

**Version:** 1.0 (2026-06-14) — Initial methodology. Will be refined as actual engagements occur.

---

## Stage 0: Sourcing & Discovery

Before we can evaluate a company, we need to find them. This stage defines how we identify candidate companies worth investigating.

### 0.1 Sourcing Channels

**Primary sources (in priority order):**

1. **Industry research agent** — Dedicated agent running research queries to identify companies fitting our criteria (e.g., "well-run but overlooked companies in construction," "operationally excellent but poor at sales")
   - Continuous; produces a steady pipeline of candidates
   - Outputs: Candidate name, industry, rough assessment of why they might fit

2. **Personal network & relationships** — Saul's existing connections, referrals from investors/operators, advisors
   - High signal (people know Saul can help)
   - Approach: Warm intro from mutual connection

3. **Industry conferences & events** — Observe companies, talk to founders, sponsors, etc.
   - Real-time signal; meet founders directly
   - Approach: Informal conversation first, then structured outreach

4. **Public data signals** — Growth patterns, hiring, news
   - Example: Company hired 50 people but revenue barely grew (ops problem)
   - Example: Founder published great writing but company is unknown (marketing problem)
   - Approach: Public research, then cold outreach

5. **Customer/vendor networks** — Talk to people who work with them
   - Example: "Company X builds an amazing product but they're terribly disorganized internally"
   - Approach: Introductions, then approach the company

### 0.2 Candidate Sourcing Checklist

For each sourced candidate, document:
- Company name, industry, founder/key person
- Revenue size (approximate)
- Reputation (known as great, unknown, mixed?)
- Why they fit our criteria (what makes them potentially great?)
- Current status (growing, stalled, shrinking?)
- Contact information for founder/leader
- Source of lead (who/how we found them)

## Stage 1: Evaluation — Is this a Good Fit?

Once sourced, evaluate the candidate against three gates: Greatness, Blocking Factor, Addressability.

### Gate 1: Greatness Screening

A "great" company has both financial health and a defensible competitive position.

#### 1.1 Financial Quality Screen

**Criteria (must meet at least 2 of 3):**

| Dimension | Threshold | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Profitability | ROE > 12% OR ROIC > WACC + 2% | Shows durable profit generation above cost of capital |
| Leverage | Net Debt / EBITDA < 2.5x OR Debt / Assets < 0.4 | Indicates financial stability; not overleveraged |
| Earnings Quality | Operating CF > 80% of Net Income | Shows earnings are real cash, not accounting artifacts |

**Decision:** If fewer than 2 of 3 are met, company fails Gate 1. Stop. Otherwise, proceed to moat screening.

#### 1.2 Competitive Moat Assessment

A "moat" is a defensible advantage that makes it hard for competitors to copy. Assess which moat(s) the company has.

**Moat Categories (score each 0-2: No/Weak/Strong):**

1. **Network Effects** — Does value increase as more people/companies use it?
   - Examples: Marketplace, platform, multi-user software
   - Strong if: Users benefit more with scale; customer switching cost is high due to network

2. **Intangible Assets** — Brand, reputation, licenses, patents, relationships
   - Examples: Trusted brand, long-term vendor relationships, proprietary process, regulatory license
   - Strong if: Customers prefer this company specifically; hard to replicate trust

3. **Cost Advantage** — Can they operate cheaper than competitors?
   - Examples: Proprietary technology, lean operations, scale, supplier relationships
   - Strong if: Structural cost advantage (not just current mgmt); competitors can't match without major investment

4. **Switching Costs** — How hard is it for customers to switch to a competitor?
   - Examples: Deep integration, custom workflows, training investment, switching cost in $$
   - Strong if: Customer would lose significant value/money by leaving

5. **Scale** — Does size itself create advantage?
   - Examples: Economies of scale in purchasing, production, distribution; market share = credibility
   - Strong if: Larger players have structural advantages; hard for smaller competitors to compete

**Decision:** Company must have at least 2 moats scored as "Strong." If 0-1 strong moats, fails Gate 1. Otherwise, proceed to Gate 2.

### Gate 2: Blocking Factor Identification

A blocking factor is a specific, identifiable reason the company isn't winning as much as they should be. It's not about lack of greatness—it's about something preventing that greatness from translating to success.

#### 2.1 Categories of Blocking Factors

**Sales & Market Access:**
- Poor sales process (no repeatable playbook, slow close, high CAC)
- Weak marketing (unknown to target market, reputation issue, no lead generation)
- Distribution problem (can't reach customers cost-effectively)
- Pricing misalignment (underpriced, or unclear value to justify price)

**Operational & Process:**
- Inefficient internal processes (manual workflows, tech debt, bottlenecks)
- Poor financial controls (unclear margins, cash flow issues, accounting chaos)
- Execution inconsistency (good product but delivery/service is unreliable)
- Scaling challenges (works at current size but breaks as they grow)

**Regulatory & Compliance:**
- Regulatory burden (permitting, licensing, compliance overhead preventing growth)
- Legal exposure (pending litigation, unclear liability, regulatory risk)
- Market entry barriers (regulatory gatekeeping, licensing requirements)

**Technology & Product:**
- Technical debt (legacy systems, slow product iteration)
- Product-market fit gaps (right direction but wrong positioning/packaging)
- Missing capability (need a specific tool/feature to compete)

**Market/Timing:**
- Market awareness (company exists but industry doesn't know about them)
- Network disadvantage (locked out of key relationships/deals)
- Category definition (market doesn't have language for what they do)

#### 2.2 How to Identify the Blocking Factor

**Primary sources (in order of effort/reliability):**

1. **Direct conversation** — Ask the founder/leader: "What's the #1 thing preventing you from winning more?"
   - Most reliable but requires access
   - Look for: A specific, repeatable problem (not "we need more capital")

2. **Public signals** — LinkedIn, interviews, press, website
   - Revenue/growth trajectory vs. apparent quality mismatch
   - Job listings (hiring for sales, ops, tech suggests internal gaps)
   - Founder/team background (e.g., great product people but no sales leader = sales weakness)
   - Industry reputation (known as great but small/slow-growing)

3. **Industry interviews** — Talk to customers, competitors, adjacent players
   - "Why don't you work with them more?"
   - "What would it take for them to grow 10x?"

4. **Proxy signals** — If no direct conversation available
   - High employee tenure (good culture/stability) but slow growth (capability gap)
   - Recurring revenue + great retention (love product) but slow new customer acquisition (sales/marketing)
   - Founder working in-house (great at craft) but no sales infrastructure (sales capability missing)

#### 2.3 Validate the Blocking Factor

Once identified, verify it's real and solvable:

**Questions to answer:**

- Is it **specific and repeatable**? (Not "we need more money" or "timing," but "we can't scale sales without better systems")
- Is it **solvable in 3-6 months**? (Not a 5-year cultural shift or fundamental product redesign)
- Would **solving it unlock growth**? (Removing the block = direct path to better financial outcomes)
- Is it **not already their core competency**? (They're not already great at sales; they're great at product, ops, delivery, etc.)

**Decision:** If all 4 are YES, proceed to Gate 3. If any is NO or unclear, the company may not be a good fit.

### Gate 3: Addressability & Opportunity Sizing

Saul's team can help with: sales efficiency, regulatory/on-ground work, operational tasks, tech debt, market access. This gate confirms the blocking factor is something they can address and worth addressing.

#### 3.1 Addressability Assessment

**Question:** Can Saul's team realistically help solve this blocking factor?

**Map to capabilities:**

| Blocking Factor | Saul's Team Can Help? | How |
|---|---|---|
| Sales process / lead gen | YES | Build sales systems, playbooks, process |
| Marketing / brand | MAYBE | Depends on scale; positioning help, but not full marketing build |
| Regulatory / permitting | YES | On-ground work, relationships, process optimization |
| Ops / processes | YES | Workflow design, automation, systems |
| Tech debt / product | YES | Engineering help, rebuild/modernize |
| Market access / network | MAYBE | Introductions, relationship building (if within network) |
| Pricing | MAYBE | Analysis + positioning help |
| Fundamental product gap | NO | They need internal product team, not external help |

**Decision:** Factor must map to "YES" or "MAYBE + clear plan." If "NO," the company doesn't fit. If "MAYBE," require a specific hypothesis for how to help.

#### 3.2 Opportunity Sizing

**Estimate the impact of solving the blocking factor:**

| Metric | How to Estimate |
|--------|---|
| **Current Revenue** | Public data, interviews, proxy (employee count × avg revenue per employee) |
| **Realistic 3-year Revenue if block is solved** | Ask founder; benchmark against peer companies; estimate from unblocked sales capacity |
| **Revenue Uplift** | 3-year revenue - current revenue |
| **Implementation Timeline** | 3-6 months for external help (ops, sales, tech); longer for regulatory (6-12 months) |
| **Team Effort** | How many people, how many weeks? |

**Quick filter:** If revenue uplift is < $1M/year or requires > 6 months and 3+ full-time people, the ROI may not justify it. (Adjust based on Saul's risk appetite.)

#### 3.3 Repeatability Check

**Question:** Can the same solution help multiple companies in similar positions?

- **High repeatability:** Sales system problem (same playbook helps 5+ sales-weak founders) → Can scale
- **Medium repeatability:** Regulatory burden in a specific state (can help others in same jurisdiction)
- **Low repeatability:** One-off tech rebuild (bespoke to that company)

**Why it matters:** Saul wants to build leverage. A blocking factor that's common (many great companies stuck on sales) is better than one that's company-specific.

### Evaluation Decision Tree

```
┌─ Candidate Company
│
├─ Gate 1: Is the company GREAT?
│  ├─ Financial quality (2+ of 3 thresholds met)? → NO → REJECT
│  ├─ Competitive moats (2+ strong)? → NO → REJECT
│  └─ → YES → Proceed to Gate 2
│
├─ Gate 2: Does it have a solvable BLOCKING FACTOR?
│  ├─ Identified a specific, repeatable problem? → NO → REJECT
│  ├─ Solvable in 3-6 months? → NO → REJECT
│  ├─ Would solving it unlock growth? → NO → REJECT
│  ├─ Is it NOT their core competency? → NO → REJECT
│  └─ → YES → Proceed to Gate 3
│
├─ Gate 3: Can we ADDRESS it?
│  ├─ Maps to sales, ops, tech, regulatory, or market access? → NO → REJECT
│  ├─ Revenue uplift > $1M/year? → NO → CONSIDER REJECT (low impact)
│  ├─ Implementation < 6 months? → NO → CONSIDER REJECT (long timeline)
│  └─ → YES → PASS EVALUATION
│
└─ ✅ PASS EVALUATION → Ready for outreach (Stage 2)
```

## Stage 2: Approach & Outreach

Once a company passes evaluation, we need to approach the founder in a way that opens the door.

### 2.1 Outreach Strategy

**Principle:** We're not pitching ourselves. We're saying "we see something great about what you're doing, and we see something blocking you. We want to help you get what you deserve."

**Approach paths (in order of preference):**

1. **Warm introduction** — Mutual connection introduces Saul to the founder
   - Highest success rate
   - Request: "I'd like to introduce you to Saul Brauns. He's helping great companies that are stuck on [specific blocking factor]."

2. **Direct reach from Saul** — Personal email to founder
   - More credible than third-party pitch
   - Formula: Observation of what they're good at → Observation of what's blocking them → "I think we can help"

3. **Team member outreach** — Someone from Saul's team makes initial contact
   - Works if relationship is specialized (e.g., regulatory expert reaches out on permitting issues)

### 2.2 Outreach Timing

**When to reach out:**

- Do NOT reach out if founder is in crisis mode (fundraising, major lawsuit, bankruptcy threat)
- Do reach out when founder has: stable revenue, functional team, time to think about improvement
- Best timing: Post-milestone (raised funding, new product launch, hired new team member)

### 2.3 Outreach Message Template

**Core message (adapt to context):**

Subject: [Observation of blocking factor]

Body:
1. **Hook:** Brief observation of what they do well (specific, not generic)
2. **The Block:** What you see preventing them from winning more
3. **The Ask:** "We help companies like you solve this. Would love 15 minutes to explore."

**Do NOT:**
- Pitch Saul's company/product yet
- Claim to have "the solution"
- Use jargon or corporate speak
- Make it about Saul's needs

## Stage 3: Value Proposition & Pitch

If they say yes to a conversation, the next step is to clarify what we actually offer and why it fits them.

### 3.1 The Core Pitch

**Framing:** "We help great companies that are stuck on [blocking factor]."

**What we offer (by blocking factor):**

| Blocking Factor | What We Offer |
|---|---|
| Sales/lead gen | Sales system design, playbook, process, training |
| Marketing | Positioning clarity, brand strategy (limited scope) |
| Operations | Process redesign, workflow optimization, training, implementation |
| Tech debt | Engineering rebuild, modernization, technical strategy |
| Regulatory | On-ground relationship work, process navigation, compliance audit |
| Market access | Introductions, network leverage, strategy |

**What we DON'T offer:**
- Capital (we're not investors)
- Long-term outsourced execution (we build capacity, then step back)
- Solutions that require internal product/engineering the company must own

### 3.2 Engagement Structure

**How we work together:**

1. **Discovery phase (1-2 weeks):** We fully understand the blocking factor, the company's constraints, what success looks like
2. **Design phase (1-2 weeks):** We design the solution (system, process, plan)
3. **Build phase (4-12 weeks):** We execute and train their team
4. **Transition phase (2-4 weeks):** We step back, they own it

**Typical engagement: 2-3 months, 2-3 people from Saul's team**

**Pricing/structure:** TBD (discuss with Saul based on scope)

### 3.3 Success Metrics

**How we define success:**
- Blocking factor is solved or significantly reduced
- Company team can own and iterate on the solution going forward
- Financial impact realized (growth, margin improvement, etc.)

## Stage 4: Engagement & Execution

Once engaged, how we work and how we ensure success.

### 4.1 Engagement Principles

1. **Company owns the outcome.** We're supporting, not running things. The founder/leadership drives decisions.
2. **Speed and focus.** We identify the top 3 changes that matter most; ignore the rest.
3. **Knowledge transfer.** Our job is to make them better at this, not to be indispensable.
4. **Regular check-ins.** Weekly or biweekly status + monthly outcome review.

### 4.2 Implementation Plan Template

For each engagement:

**Phase 1: Discovery**
- Interviews with founder, team, customers
- Document current state
- Identify root causes of blocking factor
- Define success metrics

**Phase 2: Design**
- Propose solution (process, system, tool, organizational change)
- Get approval from founder
- Build detailed implementation plan

**Phase 3: Execution**
- Build/implement with team
- Train them as we go
- Weekly check-ins, course corrections as needed

**Phase 4: Transition**
- Document processes/systems
- Ensure team can operate without us
- Gradual step-back over 2-4 weeks

### 4.3 Documentation & Knowledge Capture

**For each engagement, document:**
- What the blocking factor was
- Why it existed (root cause)
- How we solved it
- What changed (process, system, results)
- Lessons learned
- What would we do differently next time?

## Stage 5: Learning & Refinement

This methodology will evolve as we learn what works and doesn't.

### 5.1 How We Learn

**After each engagement:**
1. Capture the documentation (above)
2. Update this methodology with what we learned
3. Flag patterns: "Sales/process problems are common in X industry" or "Regulatory blocks usually require 6+ months"
4. Update criteria if needed: Do our thresholds screen for good fits? Do we miss companies we should take on?

**Quarterly review:**
1. Aggregate learnings across all engagements
2. Update sourcing strategy (which channels produce best fits?)
3. Refine decision thresholds
4. Adjust pitch based on what resonates

### 5.2 Metrics We Track

For each engagement:
- Lead source (how did we find them?)
- Pass/fail at each gate (did criteria predict good fit?)
- Blocking factor category
- Actual timeline vs. estimated
- Revenue impact (if we can measure it)
- Team satisfaction (would we do it again? What was hard?)

### 5.3 Continuous Improvement

**This document is the source of truth, but it will change.**
- When we discover a threshold doesn't work, update it
- When we find a new sourcing channel that works, add it
- When we learn a blocking factor category we missed, add it
- When we find a pitch that consistently works, document it

---

## Notes & Caveats

1. **Greatness is necessary but not sufficient.** A great company with no blocking factor (already winning) is not a candidate.

2. **Blocking factors are real constraints, not marketing angles.** The founder should recognize it; it's not something Saul has to convince them of.

3. **Timing matters.** A company at the exact moment the block becomes solvable (founder ready to improve, board aligned, budget available) is more valuable than one mid-struggle.

4. **Repeatability is a bonus, not a requirement.** A one-off high-impact opportunity is worth pursuing, but patterns enable scaling.

5. **This methodology identifies targets and engages them; it doesn't guarantee success.** Even a great company with a solvable block can fail if execution is poor or founder isn't fully bought in.
